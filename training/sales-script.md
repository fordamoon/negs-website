# Walk-In Sales Script

**For:** Matthew Alighieri
**Scenario:** Walking into a marina, inn, B&B, campground, or tour office cold
**Goal:** Start a conversation, identify pain, leave with a next step
**Time:** 5–10 minutes if they'll give it to you

---

## Before You Walk In

Know three things:

1. **Their name.** Google the business. Find the owner or GM name. Use it.
2. **One specific thing about them.** A recent review, their website, a listing they're running. Something that shows you looked.
3. **Your one-liner.** Have it ready before you open the door.

Don't bring a laptop. Don't bring a deck. Bring a business card and your phone (for the Headwaters demo if it gets that far).

---

## The Opener (30 seconds)

Walk in. Make eye contact. Be direct.

> "Hey — are you [Name]? I'm Matthew. I run New England Growth Studio up in the Lakes Region. I work with tourism and hospitality businesses on their marketing — specifically helping them figure out which channels are actually driving bookings and which ones are just burning money."

Pause. Let them react.

If they're busy or brushing you off:

> "I know you're busy — I'm not going to pitch you anything. I just wanted to introduce myself and leave you a card. If you ever want a second set of eyes on where your marketing dollars are going, I'm around."

Hand the card. Leave. Don't push. You planted a seed.

If they engage even slightly — "oh yeah?" or "we've been meaning to look at that" or even just a nod — keep going.

---

## The Bridge Question (60 seconds)

You need ONE question that opens the real conversation. Pick the one that fits:

**For marinas and boat rentals:**
> "Out of curiosity — how are most of your customers finding you right now? Is it mostly word of mouth, or are you running ads somewhere?"

**For inns and B&Bs:**
> "When you look at your bookings from last season, do you have a sense of what percentage came from direct traffic versus OTAs like TripAdvisor or Booking.com?"

**For campgrounds and outdoor operators:**
> "Are you doing any paid marketing right now, or is it mostly organic and repeat customers?"

**For any business:**
> "If I asked you which of your marketing channels drove the most revenue last year, would you be able to tell me?"

The answer to all of these is almost always some version of "I'm not really sure" or "we think it's Google but honestly we don't know." That's your opening.

---

## The Pain Point (2 minutes)

Once they admit they don't know, don't lecture. Empathize. Then name the cost.

> "Yeah — that's the most common answer I get. And honestly, it's not because people aren't paying attention. It's because the tools that answer that question have always been built for big companies with big budgets."

> "The problem is, when you don't know which channel is working, you end up funding everything equally. And in my experience, usually one or two channels are doing 70–80% of the work. Which means you're probably spending real money on channels that aren't bringing anyone through the door."

Pause. Let that land.

> "I talked to a marina owner last year who'd been spending about $3,800 a month across four channels. When we looked at the actual data, print and a local tourism directory were contributing almost nothing. He moved $1,500 a month into what was actually working and said it was the first time in 22 years he'd made a marketing decision based on data."

---

## The Tool (2 minutes)

If they're engaged, introduce Headwaters. Keep it short.

> "So I built a tool called Headwaters that does exactly this. You give it your marketing spend by channel and your booking numbers — can be a simple spreadsheet — and it runs the same kind of analysis that Fortune 500 companies use. Takes about 60 seconds. Gives you a clear breakdown of which channels are driving revenue and which ones aren't."

If they want to see it:

> "Want me to show you? I've got a demo right here that runs on sample data — takes 30 seconds."

Pull out your phone. Open getheadwaters.app/demo. Walk them through:
1. "This is what the input looks like — spend by channel, bookings by month."
2. "This is the output — see how it breaks down each channel's contribution."
3. "This number here tells you how confident the model is. The higher, the better."
4. "For your business, you'd upload your own data and get your own version of this."

---

## The Close (60 seconds)

You have three possible closes depending on how warm they are.

### Close A — They're Interested in Headwaters
> "It's $297 for the year. If it doesn't show you something useful in the first 30 days, I'll refund you completely. If you've got your marketing spend numbers and your booking records, you could have your first report by tomorrow."

Leave them the URL: **newenglandgrowthstudio.com**

### Close B — They're Interested But Not Ready to Buy
> "No rush at all. If you want a quick read on how ready your business is for this kind of thing, we've got a free 10-question assessment online — takes two minutes, gives you a score and a specific next step. No email required to see your results."

Leave them the URL: **newenglandgrowthstudio.com** (AI Readiness Score is the main CTA on the site)

### Close C — They Want to Talk More
> "Why don't we grab coffee sometime this week? I can take a look at what you're running and give you an honest read on where I think the opportunities are. No charge, no obligation. I just like talking to operators."

Get their number or email. Follow up within 24 hours. Don't wait.

---

## The Exit

Regardless of which close you land on:

> "Appreciate you giving me a few minutes. I know everyone walks in here trying to sell something. I'm not trying to sell you — I'm just trying to make sure the money you're already spending is actually working for you."

Handshake. Leave the card. Walk out.

---

## After the Visit

**Within 2 hours:**
- Log the name, business, email/phone, and notes in your CRM (or a spreadsheet if CRM isn't set up yet)
- Note their pain point and which close you used

**Within 24 hours:**
- Send a short follow-up. Text is fine if you got a cell number. Email works too. Keep it to three sentences:

> "Hey [Name] — great meeting you today. Here's that link I mentioned: newenglandgrowthstudio.com. If you want to run your numbers through Headwaters or just talk through your marketing, I'm around."

**Within 7 days:**
- If they haven't responded, one more touch. Don't be weird about it. Just:

> "Hey [Name] — following up from last week. No pressure at all — just wanted to make sure you had the link. Let me know if you want to grab that coffee."

After two touches with no response, move on. You planted the seed. They'll come back when the pain is acute enough.

---

## Rules for the Room

1. **Never badmouth their current marketing.** You don't know what's working and what isn't until you see the data. Be curious, not judgmental.

2. **Never oversell.** If Headwaters isn't right for them — maybe they only use one channel, or they have no historical data — say so. Credibility compounds.

3. **Match their energy.** If they're in a rush, be fast. If they want to chat, chat. Read the room.

4. **Don't talk about AI unless they bring it up.** "AI" is a loaded word for a lot of small business owners. Talk about data, clarity, and making better decisions. The technology is irrelevant to them — the outcome is everything.

5. **Leave every interaction with them thinking: "that guy was helpful and not pushy."** That's the brand. That's the whole thing.
