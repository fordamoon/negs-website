# YouTube Script 03 — "I Left Apple to Help Small Businesses. Here's Why."

**Channel:** New England Growth Studio (or Matthew Alighieri)
**Length:** 8–10 minutes
**Format:** Talking head, personal, storytelling
**Tone:** Honest, vulnerable, founder story — not polished, not corporate
**Goal:** Build trust, tell the origin story, create emotional connection with the audience

---

## HOOK (0:00–0:30)

Three years ago I was working in tech. Before that I was at Apple. Before that I was one of the first 300 employees at Pandora.

Today I help marina operators and inn owners in New Hampshire figure out if their TripAdvisor listing is worth the money.

That might sound like a step down. It isn't. Let me explain.

---

## SECTION 1 — THE BACKGROUND (0:30–2:30)

I've been in digital media and growth strategy for 16 years. I started at Pandora when the company was still small enough that everyone knew everyone. I watched it scale. I learned how data-driven companies make decisions — real decisions, not vibes, not opinions. They measure, they analyze, they move.

I moved to Apple. Same discipline, bigger scale. Everything optimized. Everything tracked. Decisions backed by data at every level.

And I'm not going to lie — that world was exciting. Smart people. Big problems. Real resources.

But somewhere along the way, I started noticing something. All this incredible growth infrastructure — the analytics, the modeling, the attribution tools — was only available to companies that could write seven-figure checks for software licenses.

The businesses that needed it most? The ones running on thin margins, seasonal cash flow, and gut instinct? They had nothing.

---

## SECTION 2 — THE MOVE (2:30–4:30)

I moved to New Hampshire about three years ago. I won't bore you with all the personal reasons, but part of it was wanting to build something that felt real. Something closer to the ground.

I started meeting people. Marina owners. Innkeepers. Campground operators. Fishing charter captains. People who wake up at five in the morning and don't stop until dark. People who are incredible at what they do.

And every time I'd ask about their marketing, I'd hear the same thing. "We spend a few thousand a month." "We're not sure what's working." "Our TripAdvisor rep says we're getting a lot of views." "We've been meaning to look at our Google Ads but we haven't had time."

These aren't dumb people. These are people running real businesses that employ real people in real communities. They just never had access to the tools that would tell them whether their marketing spend is working.

That gap — between what big companies know and what small businesses don't — is the reason I started New England Growth Studio.

---

## SECTION 3 — WHAT I ACTUALLY DO (4:30–6:30)

So what does this look like in practice?

The first thing I built was Headwaters. It's a marketing attribution tool — you upload your spend and your booking data, and it tells you which channels are actually driving revenue. Same math that Procter & Gamble has used for decades. But instead of $50,000 and a data team, it's $297 and a spreadsheet.

Then I built the AI Readiness Score — a free quiz that tells a business owner where they stand in terms of data, systems, and readiness for AI. Because I kept finding that some businesses weren't ready for Headwaters yet. They didn't have a CRM. They weren't tracking lead sources. They needed the foundation first.

And then there's the consulting work. The Sprint is a two-week engagement where I come in, look at everything — data, marketing, follow-up, operations — and build a 90-day roadmap. For businesses that want to go deeper, I do funnel and automation builds. And for businesses that want an ongoing growth partner, I offer a fractional role.

But the through line is the same in all of it: connect the data, see what's working, stop paying for what isn't.

---

## SECTION 4 — WHY THIS MATTERS (6:30–8:30)

I'm going to get real for a second.

Tourism is the backbone of this region. In the Lakes Region of New Hampshire — where I live — tourism isn't a nice-to-have. It's the economy. Inns, restaurants, marinas, boat rentals, campgrounds, ski areas. These businesses keep towns alive.

And they're under pressure. Costs are up. Staffing is harder. Online competition is fiercer. The big platforms keep raising prices and taking bigger cuts.

The businesses that are going to survive — and thrive — over the next five years are the ones that figure out how to be smarter with their money. Not spend more. Spend smarter. Know what's working. Automate what can be automated. Free up time for the stuff that actually matters — being on the water, talking to guests, building something real.

That's not a technology pitch. That's a survival strategy.

I didn't leave Apple because I was bored. I left because I saw a problem I could actually fix. And fixing it for a marina owner in Laconia feels more meaningful to me than optimizing a dashboard for a company that already has everything.
