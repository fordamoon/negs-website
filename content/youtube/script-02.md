## NUMBER 1 — COST PER BOOKING BY CHANNEL (0:30–3:00)

The first number is your cost per booking by channel.

Not your total marketing spend. Not your cost per click. Your cost per actual booking, broken down by where you're spending money.

Here's why this matters. Most operators I talk to can tell me their total marketing budget. They can tell me roughly how many bookings they got last season. But they can't tell me what each booking cost them on Google versus Facebook versus TripAdvisor versus email.

And without that number, you can't make a rational budget decision. You're just spreading money around and hoping.

How to get it: take your total spend on each channel for last season, divide it by the number of bookings you can attribute to that channel. If you can't attribute bookings to channels — that's the whole problem. That's what Headwaters was built to solve. But even a rough estimate is better than nothing.

What you'll often find is something like this: Google costs you $45 per booking. Email costs you $8. Facebook costs you $120. TripAdvisor costs you $90.

When you see those numbers side by side, the budget decision makes itself. You don't need a consultant. You need the number.

## NUMBER 2 — AVERAGE RESPONSE TIME TO A NEW INQUIRY (3:00–5:30)

The second number is how long it takes you to respond to a new booking inquiry.

I know this sounds basic. It isn't.

A study from Harvard Business Review — and this data has been validated across multiple industries — found that businesses that respond to a lead within the first hour are seven times more likely to qualify that lead than businesses that respond even one hour later.

Seven times.

Now think about tourism and hospitality. Someone finds your inn online. They fill out a contact form or send a message. They're probably also looking at two or three other properties. Whoever responds first doesn't just have an advantage — they often get the booking.

I've talked to operators whose average response time is 24 to 48 hours. Not because they don't care. Because they're running a physical business. They're at the dock. They're checking guests in. They're fixing a pipe.

But those 24 hours cost real money. If you get 50 inquiries a month and you're losing even 20% of them to slow follow-up, and your average booking is worth $500, that's $5,000 a month walking out the door.

How to fix it: set up an auto-response. It can be simple. "Thanks for reaching out — I'll get back to you within a few hours. In the meantime, here's our availability calendar." That one message buys you time and keeps the lead warm.

If you want to go further, automate the full follow-up sequence. That's what we build in the Funnel & Follow-Up Automation tier of our consulting. But the auto-response alone is a massive improvement.

## NUMBER 3 — REPEAT GUEST PERCENTAGE (5:30–7:30)

The third number: what percentage of your bookings come from repeat guests versus new customers?

This matters because the economics are completely different. Acquiring a new customer might cost you $80–$150 in marketing spend. Getting a past guest to come back costs you almost nothing — an email, maybe a small incentive.

If you don't know your repeat rate, you can't optimize for it. And in my experience, most seasonal businesses underinvest in their existing customer base because they're so focused on new acquisition.

Here's what I recommend. Pull your booking records from last season. Count how many customers had booked with you before. Divide by total bookings. That's your repeat rate.

If it's below 30%, you have a retention problem. Probably means you're not emailing past guests or giving them a reason to come back.

If it's above 50%, you have a goldmine. Those people already trust you. Email them first when the season opens. Give them early access. Make them feel valued. They'll fill your shoulder season.

Either way, this number should shape how you split your budget between acquisition and retention. Most operators I meet are spending 90% on new customers and 10% on past guests. For a lot of businesses, that ratio should be closer to 60/40.
