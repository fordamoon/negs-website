## SECTION 1 — THE PROBLEM (0:30–2:30)

Here's what I see every time I sit down with a tourism operator.

They're spending three, four, five thousand a month across multiple channels. Google Ads. Facebook. Maybe a TripAdvisor premium listing. Maybe some print. Maybe a local tourism directory.

And when I ask which one is working, I get one of three answers. "I think it's Google." "We're not really sure." Or my personal favorite — "TripAdvisor says our listing gets a lot of views."

Views. Not bookings. Views.

Here's the issue. Every ad platform is designed to make itself look good. Facebook will tell you it drove conversions. Google will say the same thing. They're both taking credit for the same customer. Meanwhile you're paying all of them for one booking.

And without an independent way to measure what actually happened — your real spend versus your real bookings — you're stuck making next season's budget decisions the same way you made last season's. Which is gut feel.

Gut feel works when you have three customers. It doesn't work when you're managing a $40,000 annual marketing budget.

---

## SECTION 2 — THE REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE (2:30–4:30)

Let me give you a real example.

Last summer I worked with an inn owner in the Lakes Region of New Hampshire. She was spending about $4,400 a month. Split roughly equally across Google, Facebook, and TripAdvisor. She'd been doing this for two years.

I ran her numbers through a tool I built called Headwaters. It does marketing mix modeling — same technique the big companies use — but built for small businesses. Takes about 60 seconds.

Here's what came back. Google was driving 71% of her booking revenue. TripAdvisor was at 19%. Email — which she barely used — was at 6%. And Facebook? Four percent.

She was putting a third of her budget into a channel that was driving four percent of her revenue. For two years.

When she saw the numbers, she didn't need me to tell her what to do. She cut Facebook by 75% the next week, moved the money into search and email, and finished the season with the same occupancy rate — spending $1,200 less per month.

That's $14,400 a year. From a single insight.
